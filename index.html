K12Insight: Redesining the way a user shares a survey.

Usability evaluation and redesign

  • Date: Oct- Dec 2018
  • Client: K12Insight
  • Category: UI/UX design
Introduction

As a UX/UI designer at K12 Insight, I worked as the sole designer for Engage - a survey creation tool. It is used by 3 sister companies with a completely different set of users - K12Insight (catering to school districts), Zarca interactive (corporate clients) and SoGoSurvey (DIY users).

Context

The publish module within Engage was a cause for concern due to numerous methods of sharing that the platform had. More specifically, users were unsure which sharing feature to use and when. Additionally, the feature was also cumbersome, demanding a minimum of 4 steps from our users that included several questions within. A hefty demand leading to a hefty dropout rate.

It was clear that a redesign of the sharing workflow was needed so that users could launch their surveys as seamlessly as possible.

Note: Since this was an extremely long project, I would only share the workflow of sharing by email. If you’d like to see other workflows, please reach out via email and I’d be happy to share.

Initial Goal

Make sharing of a survey easier and faster.

Research
  1. I began the research by going through the platform myself and doing the heuristic evaluation.
  2. I looked at the direct competitors like surveyMonkey, Qualtrics, SurveyGizmo, and indirect competitors like Mailchimp to see how their survey sharing feature functioned.
  3. I conducted interviews with support staff to uncover the painpoints that our users face.
  4. I then interviewed the research team who use Engage to conduct managed projects for clients. They themselves are first hand users of the product.
  5. To check the usage frequency of our various sharing methods, I dove in to the analytics provided from Google.

  3. The workflow for selecting and editing email templates had raised many complaints. Editing the template gave the impression that you could only modify the design but this is where you go to modify the content of the email as well. Design goals
  1. We wanted to make sharing surveys via email to be as familiar to the user as possible. Other more advanced features would be hidden within menus.
  2. Reducing the number of sharing options to as close to 5 as we could get. This entailed merging similar features together to lessen any redundancy.
  3. The biggest challenge in this project was simplifying the steps without losing any core functionalities that was already been in place.

Conclusion
Through the redesign of sharing options in Engage, we received highly positive reviews from the research team as well as management. The new design was able to achieve the following:
  1. Reduction in the number of sharing methods from 22 to 4, without losing any functionality.
  2. Bring more visibility on some of the hidden features like mail merge.
  3. Reduction of steps to launch a survey
  4. Removal of redundant features to improve the performance of the entire module.
Next Steps

Being a really long project, it is being shipped in phases and will be shipped entirely in a year or may take even more time.

Reflection
  1. Overall, working on this project, made me realize how keeping on adding features to a product is not a good idea. After a while, it gets too cumbersome to understand it for a new user. When thinking of adding a new feature, a lot of research needs to be done before it is shipped into the product.
  2. With such a big project, comes tight deadlines and pressure from the management. Thus keeping the management in loop at every step of the way and getting regular feedback so that you don’t derail is crucial to deliver on time.

K12Insight: Improving usability of account settings in Engage: survey making tool

Usability evaluation and redesign

  • Date: April 2019
  • Client: K12Insight
  • Category: UI/UX design
Context

In Engage: survey making tool of K12 Insight, the main account settings have too many options to choose from which takes away attention from the important settings. When it comes to finding a particular setting, it takes a user to scan through the entire side menu and make an intelligent guess to get to what they are looking for.

Current account settings overview screen
Goals
  1. Make finding relevant settings easier from the left side menu, possibly by reducing or merging similar tabs.
  2. Solve usability issues in different tabs and display the information in an intelligent manner so as to reduce the screen clutter.
  3. Show the important information in a graspable manner in tabs such as Account usage data.
  4. Improve the performance of the settings module by displaying information in a more efficient manner.
My role

Myself being the sole designer working on Engage, I was responsible for understanding the brief, identifying the usability issues, come up with creative ways to solve them and then prototype them to show how they would fit in the existing platform.

Research constraints

Since K12Insight actively discourages user research, I had to rely on other ways to do my research and making sure I am solving the right problem.

Thus for my research, I used the following resources:
  1. Asking the support team : Since these are the people directly in contact with the users and they are sort of the experts in the product, I would get their feedback first.
  2. Client complaints noted over the years : All the client complaints are documented so that nothing is overlooked.
  3. Self usability evaluation using nielsen norman heuristics, comparative analysis, conducting user interviews with employees working on different products.
Creating a poll on slack was one method to do research
Usability issues identified
  1. Many tabs have a lot of information while others have little to no information.
  2. Some tabs like Account usage data, show a lot of information but are missing important information related to limits imposed on the account.
  3. The UI elements in the overall account settings have become inconsistent over the years.

There were a lot of other small issues identified and reworked on but to keep this case study concise, I will mention some of the usability issues identified and resolved.

Account usage data

Old design of account usage data
Issues identified:
  1. Too much information presented without much emphasis on anything in particular.
  2. Information such as Responses deleted, surveys deleted, invitations deleted are not something that a user is bothered by as it does not affect their total quota.
  3. When a limit is shown, user is not sure whether it is the account limit, daily limit or the monthly limit.
Redesign of account usage data

Side navigation menu

Old design of navigation menu on the left
Issues identified:
  1. No particular order in which tabs are presented.
  2. Similar tabs like My profile, Linked Account or recent account activity and account security are not adjacent to each other.
  3. Very hard to find a tab unless you scan the complete list.
  4. The number of tabs are too many to make the user unsure of what they are looking for.
Redesigned side navigation menu

Remove Branding

Old design of remove branding setting
Issues identified:
  1. Very little information provided and the user is not sure what they are signing up for when they enable the setting.
  2. Additionally, on the screen a lot of precious real estate is wasted which can be more efficiently utilized.
Redesigned remove branding setting

Notification Center

Old design for all notifications
Issues identified:
  1. All the notifications appear in the same way and there is no way to distinguish which one in particular to pay attention to.
  2. Outdated UI style.
  3. Nothing to denote unread notifications.
  4. Time stamp is an important information and putting it on the extreme right added extra amount of burden on the user to look at it.
Notifications redesigned
Constraints
  1. I had a hard time communicating with my PM because of 3 reasons - remote communication, time zone issues (USA and India) and PM had very little understanding of UX as he was promoted to being a PM from an engineering role recently.
  2. K12Insight actively discouraged conducting user research and thus I had to look for other ways to make sure I have some substance behind my design.
Next steps

The designs were discussed with the PM team as well as the developer team to see if they could point out any loopholes or any part that we may have missed.

All the designs went live with Engage 17.0 on June 21, 2019 and can be accessed by creating an account on SoGoSurvey.

T1DAR: AR game to teach kids about Type 1 diabetes

AR game design

  • Date: June - August 2017
  • Client: Lenovo
  • Category: AR/VR Desginer
Context
  • This project came about as Phab 2 Pro, Lenovo’s new flagship phone has just come out and they were looking for content for it in order to market it better.
  • T1DAR is an augmented reality game with a purpose to teach kids suffering from Type1 diabetes about how to take care of themselves, by making them take care of a type 1 diabetic pet.
  • It is intended for kids in the age group 8-12 and suffering from Type 1 diabetes, whose parents want them to take up a sense of responsibility for their own health.
Why is it a problem?
  1. Kids suffering from Type 1 diabetes go through a lot of struggle in managing it.
  2. They have to make sure to estimate the amount of carbs in the food they are going to eat correctly and then give themselves the correct amount of insulin. Moreover, they have to do it for all the meals that they are going to eat which is too much burden for a kid less than 8 years old.
  3. The amount of insulin to be taken for a specific amount of carbs in a meal, can be very difficult to compute as well. Moreover, they have to make sure to take into account the carb ratio and correction factor as well.
  4. The educational material provided by clinicians is too long and hard to remember for kids.
  5. During the initial years of their life, their parents take care of it. But with time, the parents want the children to take responsibility of their own health.
Initial goal

To come with a prototype for a game that is fun, engaging, conveys all the learning points and can be tested with the kids at C.S.Mott children’s hospital.

My role
  1. Worked as a game designer to come up with different levels of the game. Brainstormed, sketched, storyboarded and critiqued the different ideas to come up with the design of the level.
  2. Prototyped the game in Unity going through various design sprints.
  3. Conducted usability tests and A/B tests for validation.
Objectives/success criteria
Before we started working, we decided to define some key objectives that could help guide us through the design process. Some of the main objectives were:
  1. To make the game fun and engaging.
  2. Explain the concept of carb ratio, correction factor, insulin, glucagon etc. to an 8-12 year old diabetic kid.
Research phase
  1. In order to understand more about Type 1 diabetes, we invited Dr. Joyce Lee, prof. of pediatrics in the Umich Medical school who explained us about diabetes and the common struggles associated with it.
    2. Dr. Lee explaining to us about Type 1 diabetes
  2. Apart from that we carefully observed our in-house patient, a 12 year old girl suffering from Type 1 diabetes and asked her questions at regular intervals to make sure we understood the problem clearly. We were very keen to notice how she calculated the amount of carbs in a given food and what kind of food she preferred.
    3. Observing our in-house patient on a day to day basis
  3. We performed competitive analysis, to see which all games are out there who are trying to do the same.

We worked in 2-3 week design sprints following a strict agile methodology. At the end of each sprint, we tested the prototype with a participant.

During the sprint, we started off with a brainstorming session, sketching the level design and then prototyping the same in Unity.

Introduced a timer to have a sense of urgency and a Health Bar to get an accurate feedback of their actions.

Conclusion

For conducting usability testing of the game, we tested it by making the kids play the game before we received it with the one that we received afterwards and having them take a survey. We conducted this test with 6 target (or close) users.

Our in-house patient playing the game for the first time

Survey presented to the participants

After compiling the results of the survey, we were able to increase the engagement by 2.5% and the educational value by 5%.

Next steps

We had started with the design of next level but due to time restrictions there were many things we would have liked to do but were unable to complete.

  1. Giving the users ability to do more fun things with their pet like make them more sociable or engage them in another playing activity.
    2. Dragon playing with a football

  2. Making the menu items in a semi-circular layout to denote relatedness and improve the overall UI.
    3. Semi-circular menu layout.
  3. Add an accurate visual representation of the quantity of food items throughout the game. For e.g. 1cup of Milk, 1 cup of juice, 1 bowl of watermelon, 1 tsp. of sugar etc.
Looking back or learnings from the process
  1. A game involves many complex interactions and thus it has to be prototyped (developed) in order for the person testing it to get the context right.
  2. The ability to exxplain the design changes to a designer which involves changes in their previously employed code is a very important and useful skill.
  3. Augmented reality is a very new concept and many people still don’t get the context. Thus making sure that the instructions which corresponds to the augmented reality part of the game are understood by an novice AR user, can go a long way.
  4. Hardness of a level in a game needs to be properly balanced. If too hard, the participant gives up but if too easy, it becomes uninteresting. Thus, having a level a little on the harder side makes the user remember the things taught.
  5. When aiming for a MVP, try to use and re-use as much of the interactions from the previous level as possible. This ensures consistency and make the player feel comfortable with the game. In terms of prototyping, it makes the task easier.

UM LSA Honors website

Usability evaluation

  • Date: January - April 2017
  • Client: UM LSA Honors
  • Category: Usability evaluation
Client

The Honors program is a department within the College of Literature, Science and the Arts at the University of Michigan. This department maintains the LSA Honors website, which contains information for prospective and current students, faculty and staff, and alumni.

Context

The Honors program recently shifted its website to a new Content management system. Thus the staff is forced to transfer all the information from the old website to the new website template which has caused problems in the way information is organized and presented.

This problem exacerbated when the number of emails from students unable to find information on the website; to the Honors staff increased. Thus, the staff realized the potential usability issues with the website including confusing navigation and text heaviness on some of the pages.

My role
I collaborated with 4 other students from School of Information and was actively involved in each stage of usability evaluation.
  1. Conducted interviews (as an interviewee and a note-taker)
  2. Performed comparative analysis, heuristic evaluation and usability (both acting as a moderator and a note-taker)
  3. Collaborated in the creation of a survey
Initial goals

As we started, we had the goal of making the LSA Honors website more usable and useful for current students.

Success criteria

Reduced number of emails from current students, asking about the information already listed on the website.

Scoping

Upon recommendation from the client and the current students being the major users of the website, we decided to narrow down our scope and focus only on the current students’ section.

Landing page for LSA Honors website: Current Students
Research Methodology

Method #1 : Interaction Mapping

A static representation of the LSA Honors website highlighted all the possible action states and thus any navigation issues (if 2 actions lead to the same place) with the website.

Mapping the entire LSA Honors website

Method #2 : Interviews

We conducted interviews with 5 current LSA Honors students to find out about the kind of information they look for and the methods employed by them in order to find it.

Persona based on interviews' findings

Method #3 : Comparative analysis

Through this, we wanted to see how other similar websites handle the issues related to information hierarchy, navigation structure etc.

Presentation of information by LSA Sweetland writing center website.

Method #4 : Surveys

To find quantitative data about the information seeking behavior and the overall attitude of current students towards the website. Received a total of 28 responses.

In person help was the most frequently utilized method for finding information on the LSA Honors website.

Method #5 : Heuristic evaluation

Our goal here was to identify usability issues and areas where the site could be improved. We used heuristics like Visibility of System Status, Consistency and Standards, accessibility etc. to conduct our evaluation.

Using WAVE on LSA Honors website

Using WAVE Accessibility system detects issues such as missing alt text, empty heading and links, problematic alt texts, etc.

Method #6 : Usability testing

We conducted usability tests with 5 current students to identify how several features of the website like top-level and side navigation menus, quick links, search bar, accordions, headings etc. are helping the users to find the information they are looking for.

Due to large amounts of text on pages, users used Ctrl+F (browser find functionality) to look for information.

Main findings and recommendations

Finding #1

Navigation terminology could be improved: Information about some topics could be found at more than 2 places which makes it hard for the user to remember where to find it. Also, when asked to find out about the Sophomore Honors award, all our usability test participants looked under Honors Awards and Grants page, which is understandable. However, the information is located under the Sophomore students page or the Honors requirements page.

Recommendations for finding #1

  1. More descriptive titles can be used for the pages that help the users in understanding what the page might contain. For eg.
Graduation for Honors Majors page

The Graduation for Honors Majors page talks in great detail about the process of completing an Honors Thesis, which is something that users may not be able to guess from the page title.

Finding #2

Unclear information hierarchy on a page, which makes the pages hard to skim and thus relevant information being buried in a heap of text.

  1. Many pages contain just a wall of text.
  2. Pages lack headings and subheadings.
Graduation for Honors Majors page

Recommendations for finding #2

  1. Add proper subheadings.
  2. Make use of lists, indentation and bolded text to emphasize important information.
LSA Sweetland writing center website

LSA Sweetland writing center’s website uses bold texts and headings which makes it really easy to locate information.

Finding #3

Accessibility issues - Recently, University of California Berkeley was asked to take off some of its free educational content as it was not accessible to people who were blind, deaf or hard of hearing. Read the complete article here. Owing to that, we checked the website for accessibility standards and found several issues:

  1. Alt text attribute not set for many images, thus using the website could be challenge for screen reader users.
LSA Honors website lacking alt text attribute

Using WAVE Accessibility system detects issues such as missing alt text, empty heading and links, problematic alt texts, etc.In addition, Heading levels are often skipped, and b tags are often used instead of h headings, which keeps screen reader users from skimming the webpage.

  1. Color contrast below the 4.5:1 standard. Sometimes there is low contrast between the different elements on the page.
Lack of color contrast on LSA Honors website
  1. Use of b tags for headings. Screen reader applications rely on heading tags to jump to different parts of the page. Incorrect use of heading tags decreases the skimmability of the webpages for screen reader users.
Using b html tags for headings

Recommendations for finding #3

  1. Add meaningful alt text to images. This will make sure that the website complies with the ADA (Americans with disabilities act) and make it more accessible to its diverse range of users.
  2. Using proper HTML tags for headings (h2, h3 instead of h4 and b). Adding proper HTML tags like h2, h3 rather than b, would make it easier for screen reader users to skim the webpages.
  3. Use color contrast checker to make sure the color contrast meets the standard.
Next steps
  1. Research and analyze the more frequently visited pages using Google analytics data and use these findings to inform a redesign of the website’s information hierarchy.
  2. Complete a card sorting activity, in which participants organize topics into categories that make more sense to them, to reorganize the website’s information.
Shortcomings in the study
  1. Many of our recruited participants either were associated with the LSA Honors staff or were extremely familiar with the Honors website.
  2. Even after waiting for 2 weeks, we were only able to get 28 participants to respond to our survey, out of a total of 1900. Thus, the confidence level in the results obtained was very low.
    • Could have offered a raffle to encourage people to take the survey.
    • Employed other ways like approaching faculty to ask students in their class to take the survey.
  3. All of our interviewees were freshman students which didn’t give us the complete perspective of the website usage by current students.
    • Offering participants some incentives to participate in our study could have got us a more diverse set of participants.
Looking back
  1. The timing of the survey could impact the results a lot. Since our survey was sent over the spring break, we were able to get very less responses on it.
  2. Confirming assumptions using the web analytics data is a really good idea, but it should be done earlier in the process. In our case, we got the analytics very late in the process and thus we could not look at it carefully due to time constraints and adapt accordingly.

Memorabilia: Novel social computing app concept

Product design

  • Date: September - December 2017
  • Client: None
  • Category: Product design
Context
Visiting any location, one can learn or hear about the famous things that happened there. But what about the other ordinary things that might contribute to the location history.

Memorabilia is a novel social computing application which allows people to share memories at a particular location anonymously. Our team created this app concept as a part of the course: Developing Social Computing at University of Michigan.

Why is it a problem?

The university of Michigan diag is a very popular place on the campus showcasing a big M and offers a very scenic view of the different campus buildings around it.

Figured out the problem yet?
  • This is the diag at 4 different years in the past.
  • But today when someone visits this place, they have no idea about what previously has happened in this place. And thus, they always find themselves wanting to find out more about the history of the place as to what made this place popular.
  • If you try to google search for the past of a place, the results obtained are not exactly defining of what has happened in the past at a particular location.
Initial goal

Devising and designing a novel social computing system, validating it and then developing and deploying the same on Amazon AWS.

My role
  • I collaborated with 2 other designers from the School of Information and was actively involved in each stage of the process.
  • I pitched in with sketching and designing wireframes in Balsamiq for concept testing.
  • I also took lead in the development stage and guided the other designers on how to go forward with the design in order to develop it.
Objectives
Before we started working, we decided to define some key objectives that could help guide us through the design process. Some of the main objectives were:
  1. Allow people to be able to discover random memories to inculcate a sense of serendipity.
  2. Encouraging people to know more about a location bypassing time restrictions.
  3. Encouraging conversation between random strangers.
  4. Making people discover different locations based on memories created.
Success criteria
  1. How many of the users come back to using the app?
  2. How many users sign up to start using the app?
Research phase
  1. Looked at movie trailers of movies like Harry potter and The Lake House, for inspiration related to how to depict memories.
  2. Looked at big competitors like instagram, snapchat to see how they support sharing memories using geotagging. We found our closest competitor in Drop Messages.
  3. Observational interviews with college students (most accessible and form a substantial chunk of users of social media apps) to find out how people currently think about memories in general.
Working on the affinity wall
Main findings from the affinity wall:
  1. Location is worth a 1000 stories.
  2. People want to review selective memories (filters)
  3. Personal (unusual,identity) experiences matter
  4. Memories are multifaceted (moments, long term, different representations)
  5. Sharing is depending on relevance/relation (strangers/friends) and context.

Thus we began brainstorming into potential ideas for sketching and came up with a preliminary set of sketches to lay out the design of the idea.

Concept testing

We tested the main features of the app by showing users wireframes and asking questions like interesting features, kind of memories, sharing memories with anonymous users.

Wireframes of the main features of the app

Based on the concept testing, feedback from other designers and considering the timeline of the project, we defined the scope to reach the MVP.

Thus we focussed on 3 main features of the app, that covers and explains the important aspects.

Feature #1: Ability to create a memory at any location
Create a memory screen

This feature allows users to create a memory at a particular location which can be accessed by anonymous users. We also gave the users ability to tag the memories as from our research we found that: Memories are multifaceted (moments, long term, different representations).

Feature #2: Discover memories at a particular location
Memories found at North Quad

From our research we found that: Location is worth a 1000 stories. So in order to incorporate that as well as being the USP of our app, this feature allows users to access memories created at a specific location by anonymous users.

Feature #3: Collection of memories created by a user over time at different locations
All memories created by a user

Based on our research, personal (unusual, identity) experiences matter. Thus another important feature we added was to allow the user to have a look at all the memories created by them throughout their lifetime.
